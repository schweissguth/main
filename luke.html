Luke 1 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Purpose of the Account

A man named Luke states that many people have already written reports about recent events.
He explains that he has investigated the information carefully from the beginning and is preparing an orderly account for a man named Theophilus.

2. Background on Zechariah and Elizabeth

A priest named Zechariah and his wife Elizabeth are described as obedient to God’s commands.
Both are elderly, and Elizabeth has not been able to conceive.

3. Temple Incident

During Zechariah’s assigned rotation, he is chosen by lot to burn incense inside the sanctuary.
While he is inside, he reports seeing a figure who identifies himself as an angel named Gabriel.

Gabriel delivers the following information:

  • Elizabeth will conceive a son.
  • The child is to be named John.
  • The child will have a significant role in turning people toward God.

Zechariah questions the message due to his and his wife’s age.
Gabriel states that Zechariah will be unable to speak until the events occur.

Zechariah exits the sanctuary unable to speak.
Observers conclude he has seen a vision.

4. Elizabeth’s Pregnancy

Elizabeth becomes pregnant and remains in seclusion for five months.

5. Announcement to Mary

Six months into Elizabeth’s pregnancy, Gabriel appears to a woman named Mary in Nazareth.

Gabriel provides the following information:

  • Mary will conceive a son.
  • The child is to be named Jesus.
  • The child will be given the throne of David.
  • His kingdom will not end.

Mary asks how this will occur since she is not yet living with her husband.
Gabriel states that the conception will occur through the Holy Spirit.
He also informs her of Elizabeth’s pregnancy as supporting evidence.

Mary states she accepts the message.

6. Mary Visits Elizabeth

Mary travels to Elizabeth’s home in the hill country of Judea.

Upon Mary’s arrival, Elizabeth reports that her unborn child moves strongly.
Elizabeth speaks words affirming Mary’s pregnancy and its significance.

Mary responds with a spoken declaration about God’s actions.
Mary stays with Elizabeth for about three months and then returns home.

7. Birth of John

Elizabeth gives birth to a son.
Neighbors and relatives express joy at the event.

On the eighth day, during the circumcision ceremony, people assume the child will be named after Zechariah.
Elizabeth states the name is John.
Zechariah writes the same name on a tablet.
Immediately afterward, Zechariah regains his ability to speak.

News of the event spreads through the region, and people discuss what the child may become.

8. Zechariah’s Prophecy

After regaining speech, Zechariah makes a public statement describing:

  • God’s actions on behalf of Israel
  • The role his son John will play in preparing people
  • The coming of one who will guide people out of darkness

9. John’s Early Life

The child grows and becomes strong.
He lives in remote areas until the time he begins his public work.

 

 

 

Luke 2 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Census Order

A decree is issued by Caesar Augustus requiring a census of the entire Roman world.
People are required to return to their ancestral towns for registration.

2. Travel to Bethlehem

Joseph, who is descended from David, travels from Nazareth in Galilee to Bethlehem in Judea.
Mary, who is expecting a child, accompanies him.

3. Birth of Jesus

While in Bethlehem, the time comes for Mary to give birth.
She delivers a son.
The child is placed in a feeding trough because there is no available guest lodging.

4. Shepherds and Angelic Announcement

Location: Fields near Bethlehem
A group of shepherds is working at night.

An angel appears and provides the following information:

  • A child has been born in Bethlehem.
  • The child is identified as the Messiah and Lord.
  • The sign to confirm the message is that the child will be found wrapped in cloths and lying in a feeding trough.

A group of additional angels appears briefly, giving praise to God, then departs.

5. Shepherds Visit the Child

The shepherds travel to Bethlehem and locate Mary, Joseph, and the child.
They report the message they received.
People who hear the report express interest.
Mary keeps the information in mind.

The shepherds return to their fields, speaking positively about what they witnessed.

6. Circumcision and Naming

On the eighth day, the child is circumcised.
He is given the name Jesus, consistent with the message previously delivered to Mary by the angel.

7. Presentation at the Temple

Location: Jerusalem
Joseph and Mary bring the child to the Temple to carry out required purification and dedication practices according to the Law of Moses.

They offer the sacrifice permitted for families of limited means.

8. Simeon’s Identification of the Child

A man named Simeon, described as devout and waiting for Israel’s consolation, arrives at the Temple.
He states that the Holy Spirit had indicated he would see the Messiah before his death.

Upon seeing the child, Simeon:

  • Takes the child in his arms.
  • States that he has now seen God’s salvation.
  • Predicts the child will cause both rising and falling among many in Israel.
  • Tells Mary that a figurative “sword” will pierce her own soul.

9. Anna’s Confirmation

A prophetess named Anna, elderly and known for constant worship and fasting, approaches at that moment.
She gives thanks to God and speaks about the child to those awaiting redemption in Jerusalem.

10. Return to Nazareth

After completing all required actions in Jerusalem, the family returns to Nazareth in Galilee.
The child grows physically and becomes strong, showing increasing wisdom.

11. Visit to Jerusalem at Age Twelve

When Jesus is twelve, the family travels to Jerusalem for the Passover festival.

After the festival ends, the group departs, but Jesus remains in Jerusalem without his parents’ knowledge.
Assuming he is with the traveling group, they continue for one day before searching for him.

After failing to locate him among relatives and acquaintances, they return to Jerusalem.

12. Finding Jesus in the Temple

After three days, they find Jesus in the Temple courts.
He is sitting among the teachers, listening and asking questions.
Observers note his understanding and responses.

Mary asks why he stayed behind.
Jesus states that he needed to be in his Father’s house.
His parents do not fully understand the statement.

13. Return to Nazareth and Development

Jesus returns with his parents to Nazareth and is obedient to them.
Mary keeps these events in mind.
Jesus continues to grow in wisdom, stature, and favor with God and people.

 

 

Luke 3 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Historical Setting

During the rule of Tiberius Caesar, with the following officials in place:

  • Pontius Pilate governing Judea
  • Herod governing Galilee
  • Philip governing Iturea and Trachonitis
  • Lysanias governing Abilene
  • Annas and Caiaphas serving in high priestly roles

The word of God comes to John, son of Zechariah, in the wilderness.

2. John’s Public Activity

John travels throughout the Jordan region.
He announces a baptism connected to repentance and forgiveness of sins.

His activity is described as fulfilling earlier prophetic statements about:

  • Preparing a way for the Lord
  • Making paths straight
  • Removing obstacles
  • Revealing God’s salvation to all people

3. John’s Instructions to the Crowds

Crowds come to be baptized.
John issues warnings about relying on ancestry for spiritual status.
He states that genuine repentance must be demonstrated through actions.

Specific instructions recorded:

  • People with surplus clothing or food should share with those lacking.
  • Tax collectors should collect only the required amount.
  • Soldiers should avoid extortion, false accusations, and should be content with their wages.

4. Public Speculation About John

People begin to question whether John might be the Messiah.

John responds with the following clarifications:

  • He baptizes with water.
  • One more powerful is coming.
  • That individual will baptize with the Holy Spirit and fire.
  • That individual will separate the righteous from the unrighteous.

John continues giving various exhortations to the people.

5. John’s Imprisonment

Herod, ruler of Galilee, becomes offended by John’s statements regarding his marriage to Herodias and other actions.
Herod has John imprisoned.

6. Baptism of Jesus

During a period when many people are being baptized, Jesus is also baptized.

After the baptism:

  • Jesus is praying.
  • Heaven is described as opening.
  • The Holy Spirit descends on him in a visible form resembling a dove.
  • A voice from heaven identifies Jesus as God’s Son.

7. Genealogy of Jesus

A genealogy is provided tracing Jesus’ lineage.
It begins with Joseph (identified as Jesus’ legal father) and continues through numerous generations.
The line extends back through David, Abraham, and ultimately to Adam, who is described as being created by God.

 

 

Luke 4 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Departure to the Wilderness

Jesus leaves the Jordan region full of the Holy Spirit.
He is led into the wilderness, where he remains for forty days.
During this period, he does not eat.
He experiences direct testing from the devil.

2. Three Recorded Temptations

Temptation 1: Food

The devil suggests Jesus turn a stone into bread.
Jesus declines, citing reliance on God’s word.

Temptation 2: Authority

The devil shows Jesus various kingdoms and offers authority over them in exchange for worship.
Jesus refuses, stating that worship belongs to God alone.

Temptation 3: Temple

The devil brings Jesus to the highest point of the Temple and suggests he jump, claiming angels would protect him.
Jesus refuses, stating that God is not to be tested.

After these events, the devil departs until a later time.

3. Return to Galilee

Jesus returns to Galilee in the power of the Spirit.
Reports about him spread throughout the region.
He teaches in synagogues and receives positive attention.

4. Visit to Nazareth

Jesus travels to Nazareth, where he was raised.
On the Sabbath, he enters the synagogue and reads from the scroll of Isaiah.

He reads a passage describing:

  • Good news for the poor
  • Freedom for captives
  • Sight for the blind
  • Release for the oppressed
  • A proclamation of God’s favor

He states that the passage is fulfilled in their hearing.

Initial reactions are positive, but questions arise about his background.

Jesus references two historical examples (Elijah and Elisha) where God’s actions benefited non-Israelites.
The synagogue audience becomes angry.
They escort him to a hill intending to throw him off.
He passes through the crowd and leaves the area.

5. Activity in Capernaum

Location: Capernaum, Galilee
Jesus teaches in the synagogue.
People note the authority of his teaching.

A man with an unclean spirit cries out.
Jesus commands the spirit to leave.
The spirit departs without harming the man.
Observers express surprise at Jesus’ authority.

Reports about him spread further.

6. Healing of Simon’s Mother‑in‑Law

Jesus goes to Simon’s home.
Simon's mother‑in‑law has a high fever.
Jesus rebukes the fever, and it leaves her.
She immediately resumes normal activity.

7. Evening Healings

At sunset, people bring various sick individuals to Jesus.
He lays hands on each person and heals them.
Demons come out of some individuals, identifying Jesus as the Son of God.
Jesus silences them and does not permit them to speak.

8. Early Morning Departure

At daybreak, Jesus goes to a solitary place.
Crowds search for him and attempt to keep him from leaving.

Jesus states that he must preach the kingdom of God in other towns as well.
He continues traveling and teaching in synagogues throughout the region.

 

 

Luke 5 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Teaching by the Lake

Jesus is standing beside the Lake of Gennesaret.

A crowd gathers, making it difficult for him to speak to them directly.

He enters a boat belonging to Simon and asks him to move it slightly offshore.

Jesus teaches the crowd from the boat.

 

2. Large Catch of Fish

After speaking, Jesus instructs Simon to move into deeper water and lower the nets.

Simon reports that they caught nothing during the night but agrees to follow the instruction.

The nets fill with a large number of fish and begin to tear.

They signal partners in another boat to assist.

Both boats become heavily loaded with fish.

Simon responds by expressing unworthiness.

Jesus tells him he will now be involved in “catching people,” meaning gathering followers.

Simon, James, and John bring their boats to shore and leave their work to follow Jesus.

 

3. Healing of a Man With Leprosy

A man with leprosy approaches Jesus and requests healing.

Jesus touches the man and states that he is willing to heal him.

The leprosy leaves immediately.

Jesus instructs the man to tell no one, but to show himself to a priest and offer the required sacrifice.

Despite this, news spreads, and large crowds gather.

Jesus frequently withdraws to solitary places to pray.

 

4. Healing of a Paralyzed Man

Location: A house in an unspecified town

Pharisees and teachers of the law are present.

A group attempts to bring a paralyzed man to Jesus but cannot enter due to the crowd.

They go onto the roof, remove tiles, and lower the man on a mat.

Jesus states that the man’s sins are forgiven.

Religious leaders question the statement internally.

Jesus then instructs the man to stand, pick up his mat, and go home.

The man immediately stands and leaves.

Observers express amazement.

 

5. Calling of Levi

Jesus sees a tax collector named Levi at his booth and tells him to follow.

Levi leaves his position and follows Jesus.

Levi hosts a large banquet for Jesus.

Many tax collectors and others attend.

Pharisees and their associates question why Jesus eats with such people.

Jesus responds that his mission is directed toward those who need spiritual help.

 

6. Question About Fasting

People ask why Jesus’ disciples do not fast like the disciples of John and the Pharisees.

Jesus explains that fasting is not appropriate while he is present with them but will be appropriate later.

He provides two brief illustrations:

• New cloth is not used to patch old garments.

• New wine is not placed into old wineskins.

Both statements indicate incompatibility between old practices and the new situation.

 

 

Luke 6 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Grainfields on the Sabbath

Jesus and his disciples walk through grainfields on the Sabbath.
The disciples pick heads of grain and eat them.
Some Pharisees state that this action violates Sabbath regulations.
Jesus responds that David once ate consecrated bread when hungry and concludes that the Son of Man has authority over the Sabbath.

2. Healing on the Sabbath

Location: A synagogue
A man with a withered hand is present.
Religious leaders observe Jesus to see if he will heal on the Sabbath.

Jesus instructs the man to stand.
He asks the observers whether it is lawful to do good or harm on the Sabbath.
He tells the man to stretch out his hand.
The hand is restored.
The leaders become angry and discuss what to do about Jesus.

3. Selection of the Twelve

Jesus goes to a mountain and spends the night in prayer.
In the morning, he selects twelve men from among his disciples and designates them as apostles.

The twelve are listed as:
Simon (called Peter), Andrew, James, John, Philip, Bartholomew, Matthew, Thomas, James son of Alphaeus, Simon called the Zealot, Judas son of James, and Judas Iscariot.

4. Large Crowd and Healings

Jesus comes down to a level area.
A large crowd from Judea, Jerusalem, and coastal regions gathers.
People come to hear him and to be healed.
Those with unclean spirits are cured.
Many attempt to touch him because power is coming from him and healing them.

5. Teachings Directed to Disciples

Jesus speaks to his disciples with the following statements:

Blessings

He identifies blessings for:

  • The poor
  • The hungry
  • Those who weep
  • Those who are hated because of association with him

Warnings

He identifies warnings for:

  • The rich
  • The well-fed
  • Those who laugh now
  • Those spoken well of by everyone

6. Instructions on Conduct

Jesus gives several directives:

  • Love enemies.
  • Do good to those who oppose you.
  • Bless those who curse you.
  • Offer the other cheek when struck.
  • Give to those who ask.
  • Treat others as you want to be treated.

He states that doing good only to those who do good in return has no special merit.

He instructs followers to be merciful as God is merciful.

7. Warnings About Judgment

Jesus states:

  • Do not judge or condemn.
  • Forgive others.
  • Give generously; the measure used will be returned.

He uses brief illustrations:

  • A blind person cannot lead another blind person.
  • A student is not above the teacher.
  • Remove one’s own faults before addressing another’s.

8. Tree and Fruit Analogy

Jesus states that:

  • Good trees produce good fruit.
  • Bad trees produce bad fruit.
  • A person’s words reflect what is in the heart.

9. Parable of the Two Builders

Jesus describes two types of builders:

  1. Builder who digs deep and lays foundation on rock
    • Represents those who hear and follow his teachings.
    • House withstands floods.
  2. Builder who constructs without foundation
  • Represents those who hear but do not follow.
  • House collapses when floodwaters strike

 

 

 

Luke 7 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Healing of the Centurion’s Servant

Location: Capernaum
A centurion has a servant who is seriously ill and near death.
The centurion sends Jewish elders to request that Jesus come and heal the servant.

The elders state that the centurion is supportive of the Jewish community and built their synagogue.
Jesus begins traveling toward the centurion’s home.

Before Jesus arrives, the centurion sends friends with a message stating:

  • He does not consider himself worthy for Jesus to enter his home.
  • He believes Jesus can heal by speaking a command from a distance.

Jesus notes the centurion’s level of faith.
When the messengers return home, the servant is found to be healed.

2. Raising of the Widow’s Son

Location: Town of Nain
As Jesus approaches the town gate, a funeral procession is leaving.
The deceased is the only son of a widow.

Jesus tells the woman not to weep.
He touches the stretcher and instructs the young man to rise.
The man sits up and begins to speak.
Jesus gives him back to his mother.

Observers express fear and state that God has acted among them.
Reports spread throughout the region.

3. John the Baptist’s Inquiry

John the Baptist, who is in prison, sends two disciples to ask Jesus if he is the expected one.

When they arrive, Jesus is engaged in healing diseases, casting out spirits, and restoring sight to the blind.

Jesus instructs them to report what they have seen:

  • The blind see
  • The lame walk
  • Lepers are cleansed
  • The deaf hear
  • The dead are raised
  • Good news is announced to the poor

He adds that those who do not stumble over him are blessed.

After the messengers leave, Jesus speaks to the crowd about John:

  • John is identified as the messenger predicted in earlier writings.
  • Jesus states that no one born of women is greater than John, yet the least in God’s kingdom is greater than John.

The general population and tax collectors acknowledge God’s way by accepting John’s baptism.
Pharisees and legal experts reject it.

4. Comparison of the Generation

Jesus states that the people of the current generation are like children who complain regardless of the tune played.
He notes:

  • John came with strict practices, and people said he had a demon.
  • Jesus came eating and drinking, and people called him a glutton and a drunkard.
    He concludes that wisdom is shown to be right by its results.

5. Anointing by a Woman in a Pharisee’s House

Location: Home of a Pharisee named Simon
Jesus is invited to dine at Simon’s house.

A woman known in the town as a sinner enters the house.
She stands behind Jesus, weeping, and wets his feet with her tears.
She wipes them with her hair, kisses them, and pours perfume on them.

Simon questions internally why Jesus would allow this.

Jesus tells a brief story about two debtors forgiven different amounts.
He asks Simon which debtor would be more grateful.
Simon answers correctly: the one forgiven more.

Jesus contrasts Simon’s lack of hospitality with the woman’s actions:

  • Simon did not provide water, a kiss, or oil.
  • The woman provided tears, kisses, and perfume.

Jesus states that the woman’s many sins are forgiven.
He tells her that her faith has saved her and that she may go in peace.

Other guests question Jesus’ authority to forgive sins.

 

 

Luke 8 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Women Supporting Jesus’ Ministry

Jesus travels through towns and villages announcing the kingdom of God.
The twelve disciples accompany him.
Several women who had been healed of illnesses or freed from evil spirits also accompany him and provide material support.
Named individuals include Mary Magdalene, Joanna, and Susanna.

2. Parable of the Sower

A large crowd gathers from multiple towns.
Jesus presents a parable describing a farmer scattering seed on four types of ground:

  • Pathway
  • Rocky ground
  • Thorn‑covered ground
  • Good soil

Later, Jesus explains privately to his disciples that the seed represents God’s message and the soils represent different responses.

3. Purpose of Parables

Jesus states that parables reveal truth to some while obscuring it from others.
He identifies the “good soil” as those who hear the message, retain it, and produce results.

4. Lamp Illustration

Jesus states that a lamp is not hidden but placed where it provides light.
He adds that hidden things will eventually be revealed and that those who listen carefully will receive more understanding.

5. Jesus’ Mother and Brothers

Jesus’ mother and brothers arrive but cannot reach him due to the crowd.
When informed, Jesus states that those who hear and do God’s word are his true family.

6. Calming the Storm

Jesus and his disciples travel by boat.
Jesus falls asleep.
A storm develops, and the boat begins taking on water.
The disciples wake him.
Jesus rebukes the wind and waves, and the storm stops.
He questions the disciples’ faith.
They express concern about his authority over nature.

7. Healing of the Gerasene Demoniac

Location: Region of the Gerasenes
Upon arrival, a man possessed by many demons approaches Jesus.
The man lives among tombs and is uncontrollable.

The demons request not to be sent into the abyss.
They ask to enter a herd of pigs.
Jesus permits this.
The demons enter the pigs, and the herd runs into the lake and drowns.

Local residents observe the formerly possessed man in a normal state.
They become afraid and ask Jesus to leave the area.
The healed man requests to accompany Jesus.
Jesus instructs him to return home and report what God has done.

8. Healing of a Woman and Raising of a Girl

Upon returning to Galilee, Jesus is met by a crowd.

Jairus’ Request

A synagogue leader named Jairus asks Jesus to come to his house because his daughter is dying.
Jesus agrees and begins traveling with him.

Woman With Chronic Bleeding

A woman with a twelve‑year bleeding condition touches Jesus’ garment.
She is immediately healed.
Jesus asks who touched him.
The woman identifies herself.
Jesus states that her faith has resulted in healing.

Report of the Girl’s Death

A messenger arrives stating that Jairus’ daughter has died.
Jesus instructs Jairus not to fear but to believe.

Raising of the Girl

At the house, Jesus allows only the parents and three disciples inside.
He states that the girl is not dead but asleep.
People laugh at the statement.
Jesus takes the girl’s hand and tells her to rise.
She stands up immediately.
He instructs the parents to give her food and to keep the event private.

 

 

Luke 9 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Mission of the Twelve

Jesus calls the twelve disciples together.

He gives them authority to drive out demons and cure diseases.

He instructs them to proclaim God’s kingdom and heal the sick.

Operational instructions:

• Take no staff, bag, bread, money, or extra clothing.

• Stay in one house per town.

• If not welcomed, leave and shake the dust off their feet as a testimony.

The disciples travel through villages, preaching and healing.

 

2. Herod’s Reaction

Herod the tetrarch hears reports about Jesus.

He is confused because some say John has been raised, others say Elijah has appeared, and others say a prophet has returned.

Herod states he executed John and seeks to see Jesus.

 

3. Feeding of the Five Thousand

The apostles return and report their activities.

Jesus takes them to a remote area near Bethsaida.

Crowds follow.

Jesus teaches them and heals those needing care.

Late in the day, the disciples suggest dismissing the crowd to find food and lodging.

Jesus instructs them to feed the crowd themselves.

They report having five loaves and two fish.

Jesus organizes the people into groups.

He blesses the food, breaks it, and gives it to the disciples to distribute.

Everyone eats and is satisfied.

Twelve baskets of leftovers are collected.

 

4. Peter’s Confession

While Jesus is praying privately with his disciples nearby, he asks them who the crowds say he is.

They report various opinions: John the Baptist, Elijah, or another prophet.

Jesus asks for their own assessment.

Peter states that Jesus is the Messiah.

Jesus instructs them not to tell anyone.

 

5. Prediction of Suffering

Jesus states that:

• He will suffer, be rejected, be killed, and rise on the third day.

• Anyone who follows him must deny themselves and take up their cross daily.

• Those who try to save their life will lose it; those who lose their life for him will save it.

He adds that some present will see God’s kingdom before they die.

 

6. The Transfiguration

About eight days later, Jesus takes Peter, James, and John up a mountain to pray.

During prayer:

• Jesus’ appearance changes.

• His clothing becomes bright.

• Moses and Elijah appear and speak with him about his upcoming departure in Jerusalem.

Peter suggests building three shelters, not understanding the situation.

A cloud forms and covers them.

A voice from the cloud identifies Jesus as God’s Son and instructs them to listen to him.

After the event, they keep the matter private.

 

7. Healing a Boy With an Unclean Spirit

The next day, a crowd meets Jesus.

A man reports that his son is possessed by a spirit that causes seizures.

The disciples were unable to heal him.

Jesus rebukes the spirit and heals the boy.

Observers are impressed.

 

8. Second Prediction of Suffering

Jesus tells his disciples that he will be handed over to men.

They do not understand and are afraid to ask for clarification.

 

9. Dispute About Greatness

The disciples argue about who among them is greatest.

Jesus places a child beside him and states that receiving a child in his name is equivalent to receiving him and the one who sent him.

He states that the least among them is the greatest.

 

10. Unauthorized Exorcist

John reports that someone was driving out demons in Jesus’ name but was not part of their group.

Jesus instructs them not to stop the person, stating that anyone not against them is for them.

 

11. Samaritan Village Rejection

As Jesus heads toward Jerusalem, he sends messengers ahead to a Samaritan village.

The village does not receive him because he is traveling toward Jerusalem.

James and John ask if they should call down fire.

Jesus rebukes them, and they move on.

 

12. Three Encounters on the Road

Three individuals express interest in following Jesus.

Responses:

• Jesus states that following him may involve having no permanent lodging.

• He instructs another to prioritize proclaiming God’s kingdom over family obligations.

• He states that anyone who looks back after committing to the work is not fit for the kingdom.

 

 

Luke 10 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Appointment of the Seventy‑Two

Jesus appoints seventy‑two additional followers and sends them out in pairs to towns he plans to visit.

Instructions provided:

  • The harvest is large; workers are few.
  • Travel with no purse, bag, or sandals.
  • Offer peace to each house entered.
  • Stay in one home per town and accept provided food.
  • Heal the sick and announce that God’s kingdom has come near.
  • If a town rejects them, they are to wipe off its dust as a warning.

Jesus states that towns rejecting the message will face consequences more severe than those of ancient cities known for wrongdoing.

2. Return of the Seventy‑Two

The seventy‑two return and report successful outcomes, including authority over demons.
Jesus states that he observed Satan’s downfall and confirms their authority over harmful forces.
He instructs them to prioritize rejoicing that their names are recorded in heaven.

3. Jesus’ Prayer of Thanks

Jesus prays, stating that God has revealed truths to the humble rather than the wise.
He notes that only the Son fully knows the Father and vice versa, and that the Son reveals the Father to whom he chooses.

He tells his disciples that many prophets and kings desired to see what they are witnessing but did not.

4. The Expert in the Law

A legal expert asks Jesus what must be done to inherit eternal life.
Jesus asks him to summarize the law.
The expert states: love God fully and love one’s neighbor as oneself.
Jesus confirms the answer.

The expert asks for clarification on the definition of “neighbor.”

5. Parable of the Good Samaritan

Jesus provides a scenario:

  • A man traveling from Jerusalem to Jericho is attacked, robbed, and left injured.
  • A priest passes by without helping.
  • A Levite also passes by without helping.
  • A Samaritan stops, treats the man’s wounds, transports him to an inn, and pays for his care.

Jesus asks who acted as a neighbor.
The expert identifies the one who showed mercy.
Jesus instructs him to do likewise.

6. Visit to Martha and Mary

Location: A village home
A woman named Martha hosts Jesus.
Her sister Mary sits and listens to Jesus’ teaching.

Martha becomes concerned about the workload and asks Jesus to instruct Mary to help.
Jesus states that Mary has chosen the better focus and that it will not be taken from her.

 

 

Luke 11 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Teaching on Prayer

Jesus finishes praying in a certain place.
A disciple asks him to teach them to pray as John taught his followers.

Jesus provides a model prayer including:

  • Honor for God’s name
  • Request for God’s kingdom
  • Request for daily provision
  • Request for forgiveness, linked to forgiving others
  • Request for protection from temptation

2. Illustration About Persistence

Jesus gives an example involving a man who asks a friend for bread at midnight.
The friend initially refuses due to the late hour.
Jesus states that persistence will result in the friend eventually providing what is needed.

He adds:

  • Ask, and it will be given.
  • Seek, and you will find.
  • Knock, and the door will be opened.

He states that God gives the Holy Spirit to those who ask.

3. Accusation of Driving Out Demons by Satan

Jesus drives out a demon from a mute man.
The man begins to speak.
Some observers claim Jesus uses Satan’s power.
Others demand a sign from heaven.

Jesus responds:

  • A divided kingdom cannot stand.
  • If he drives out demons by Satan, then their own exorcists must do the same.
  • If he drives out demons by God’s power, then God’s kingdom has arrived.

He adds an illustration about a strong man guarding his house and a stronger man overcoming him.

He states that those not with him are against him.

4. Return of an Unclean Spirit

Jesus describes an unclean spirit leaving a person, wandering, and returning with seven others if it finds the place empty.
He states that the final condition becomes worse than the first.

5. True Blessedness

A woman in the crowd calls out that Jesus’ mother is blessed.
Jesus responds that those who hear and obey God’s word are blessed.

6. Demand for a Sign

Jesus states that the current generation seeks a sign but will receive only the “sign of Jonah.”
He explains:

  • Jonah was a sign to Nineveh.
  • The Son of Man will be a sign to this generation.

He states that:

  • The queen of the South will rise in judgment because she sought Solomon’s wisdom.
  • The people of Nineveh will rise in judgment because they repented at Jonah’s message.
  • Someone greater than both is present.

7. Lamp Illustration

Jesus states that a lamp is placed where it provides light.
He adds that the eye is the lamp of the body.
A healthy eye results in light; an unhealthy eye results in darkness.
He instructs listeners to ensure their inner light is not darkness.

8. Meal With a Pharisee

A Pharisee invites Jesus to dine.
Jesus does not perform the customary hand‑washing ritual.
The Pharisee is surprised.

Jesus states that Pharisees clean the outside but neglect internal matters such as justice and love.
He issues several statements of woe against Pharisees for:

  • Focusing on minor tithing while neglecting justice
  • Seeking prominent seats
  • Being like unmarked graves

A legal expert objects.
Jesus issues additional statements of woe against legal experts for:

  • Burdening people with difficult requirements
  • Honoring prophets their ancestors killed
  • Hindering others from entering knowledge of God

The religious leaders begin opposing him strongly and attempt to trap him in his words.

 

 

Luke 12 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Warning About Hypocrisy

A large crowd gathers, creating congestion.
Jesus tells his disciples to avoid the hypocrisy of the Pharisees.
He states that hidden things will eventually be revealed and that private statements will become known publicly.

2. Fear of God, Not People

Jesus instructs them not to fear those who can only kill the body.
He states that God has authority over both life and judgment.
He notes that God is aware of even small creatures and that his followers are valued.

3. Acknowledging the Son of Man

Jesus states that those who acknowledge him before others will be acknowledged before God’s angels.
Those who deny him will be denied.
He adds that speaking against the Son of Man can be forgiven, but blasphemy against the Holy Spirit will not.
He tells them not to worry about their defense when arrested; the Holy Spirit will instruct them.

4. Parable of the Rich Fool

A man asks Jesus to settle an inheritance dispute.
Jesus declines and warns against greed.

He tells a parable:

  • A rich man has a large harvest.
  • He decides to build bigger storage barns.
  • He plans to relax and enjoy his wealth.
  • God calls him a fool because he will die that night, leaving his wealth behind.

Jesus states that storing wealth for oneself without being rich toward God is unwise.

5. Do Not Worry

Jesus instructs his disciples not to worry about food or clothing.
He states that life is more than material needs.
He references birds and flowers as examples of God’s provision.
He states that worrying does not add time to life.

He instructs them to seek God’s kingdom first, and their needs will be met.

6. Treasure in Heaven

Jesus tells them to sell possessions and give to the poor.
He states that treasure in heaven is secure.
He adds that a person’s heart follows their treasure.

7. Be Ready for the Master’s Return

Jesus instructs them to be ready, like servants waiting for their master’s return from a wedding banquet.
He states that the master will reward ready servants.

He adds that the Son of Man will come at an unexpected time.

8. Peter’s Question and the Faithful Manager

Peter asks if the teaching applies to them or to everyone.

Jesus responds with an illustration:

  • A manager entrusted with responsibility must act faithfully.
  • A faithful manager will be rewarded.
  • An unfaithful manager who abuses others will be punished.
  • Those who know their master’s will and ignore it receive greater accountability.

9. Jesus’ Mission and Division

Jesus states that he has come to bring fire and that he has a baptism (referring to future suffering) to undergo.
He states that his coming will cause division even within families.

10. Interpreting the Times

Jesus notes that people can interpret weather signs but fail to interpret current spiritual conditions.
He instructs them to settle disputes quickly to avoid legal consequences.

 

 

Luke 13 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Reports of Recent Tragedies

Some individuals inform Jesus about Galileans killed by Pilate.
Jesus states that the victims were not worse sinners than others.
He adds that unless people repent, they will likewise perish.

He also references a tower collapse in Siloam that killed eighteen people.
He repeats that the victims were not more guilty than others and again calls for repentance.

2. Parable of the Fig Tree

Jesus tells a parable:

  • A man has a fig tree that has produced no fruit for three years.
  • He instructs the caretaker to cut it down.
  • The caretaker requests one more year to fertilize and cultivate it.
  • If it still produces no fruit, it will then be removed.

3. Healing a Disabled Woman on the Sabbath

Location: A synagogue
A woman with an eighteen‑year disabling condition is present.

Jesus calls her forward, states she is freed from her condition, and places his hands on her.
She immediately stands upright.

The synagogue leader objects, stating that healing should not occur on the Sabbath.
Jesus responds that people routinely untie animals for water on the Sabbath and that the woman, being a “daughter of Abraham,” should be released from her condition.

Opponents are put to shame; the crowd reacts positively.

4. Parables of the Mustard Seed and Yeast

Jesus provides two brief comparisons:

  • God’s kingdom is like a mustard seed that grows into a large plant.
  • God’s kingdom is like yeast that spreads through a large amount of dough.

5. Teaching in Various Towns

Jesus continues traveling toward Jerusalem, teaching in towns and villages.

Someone asks whether only a few will be saved.
Jesus advises striving to enter through the narrow door.
He states that some who assume they belong will be excluded, while others from distant regions will be included.

6. Warning About Herod

Some Pharisees warn Jesus that Herod intends to kill him.
Jesus responds that he will continue his work of healing and teaching until his appointed time in Jerusalem.

He states that Jerusalem has a history of rejecting prophets and expresses that its house will be left desolate until it acknowledges him.

 

 

Luke 14 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Healing on the Sabbath at a Pharisee’s House

Jesus is invited to eat at the home of a prominent Pharisee on the Sabbath.
A man with abnormal swelling is present.

Jesus asks the legal experts and Pharisees whether it is lawful to heal on the Sabbath.
They do not answer.

Jesus heals the man and sends him away.
He then asks whether they would rescue a child or an ox that fell into a well on the Sabbath.
They do not respond.

2. Teaching on Humility

Jesus observes guests choosing places of honor at the table.
He advises selecting a lower place to avoid being moved downward by the host.
He states that those who exalt themselves will be humbled, and those who humble themselves will be exalted.

He tells the host to invite people who cannot repay—such as the poor, disabled, and blind—rather than only friends and wealthy neighbors.

3. Parable of the Great Banquet

A guest comments on the blessing of eating in God’s kingdom.
Jesus responds with a parable:

  • A man prepares a large banquet and invites many guests.
  • When the banquet is ready, the invited guests make excuses:
    • One has purchased land.
    • One has bought oxen.
    • One has recently married.
  • The host becomes angry and instructs his servant to bring in the poor, disabled, blind, and lame.
  • When space remains, the servant is told to bring in people from roads and country lanes.
  • The host states that the originally invited guests will not taste the banquet.

4. Cost of Discipleship

Large crowds travel with Jesus.
He states that following him requires prioritizing him above family and personal life.
He adds that a disciple must be willing to carry their own cross.

He provides two examples:

  • A builder estimates cost before constructing a tower.
  • A king evaluates military strength before going to war.

He concludes that anyone unwilling to give up everything cannot be his disciple.

5. Salt Illustration

Jesus states that salt losing its flavor becomes useless and is thrown away.
He adds that those with ears to hear should pay attention.

 

 

Luke 15 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Setting

Tax collectors and other socially marginalized individuals gather to hear Jesus.
Pharisees and scribes complain that Jesus welcomes such people and eats with them.
Jesus responds with three parables.

2. Parable of the Lost Sheep

Jesus describes a scenario:

  • A shepherd has one hundred sheep.
  • One sheep becomes lost.
  • The shepherd leaves the ninety‑nine in open country and searches for the lost one.
  • When he finds it, he carries it home and calls neighbors to share in his satisfaction.

Jesus states that there is more rejoicing in heaven over one sinner who repents than over ninety‑nine who do not need repentance.

3. Parable of the Lost Coin

Jesus gives a second scenario:

  • A woman has ten silver coins.
  • She loses one.
  • She lights a lamp, sweeps the house, and searches carefully.
  • When she finds it, she calls neighbors to share in her satisfaction.

Jesus states that angels rejoice when one sinner repents.

4. Parable of the Lost Son

Jesus provides a third scenario involving a family:

Younger Son’s Departure

  • A man has two sons.
  • The younger son requests his share of the inheritance.
  • The father divides the property.
  • The younger son travels to a distant country and spends his wealth irresponsibly.
  • A famine occurs, and he becomes destitute.
  • He takes a job feeding pigs and becomes severely hungry.
  • He decides to return home and ask to be treated as a hired worker.

Return and Reception

  • The father sees him approaching and runs to meet him.
  • The son begins his prepared statement.
  • The father interrupts, orders servants to bring a robe, ring, and sandals, and prepares a celebratory feast.
  • The father states that the son was “lost” and is now “found.”

Older Son’s Reaction

  • The older son returns from the field and hears music.
  • A servant explains the situation.
  • The older son becomes angry and refuses to enter.
  • The father comes out to speak with him.
  • The older son complains that he has served faithfully without receiving similar celebration.
  • The father responds that everything he has belongs to the older son, but celebrating the younger son’s return is appropriate because he was lost and is now found.

 

 

Luke 16 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Parable of the Dishonest Manager

Jesus tells a parable:

  • A rich man receives reports that his manager is wasting resources.
  • The owner informs the manager that he will be dismissed and must provide an account.
  • The manager considers his options and decides to reduce the debts of several clients to secure future hospitality.
  • One debtor’s bill is reduced from one hundred measures of oil to fifty.
  • Another’s bill is reduced from one hundred measures of wheat to eighty.

The owner commends the manager for acting shrewdly, not for dishonesty but for strategic thinking.

Jesus states that:

  • People of this world often act more shrewdly than people of the light.
  • Followers should use worldly resources in ways that produce lasting benefit.
  • Faithfulness in small matters indicates faithfulness in larger ones.
  • No one can serve both God and wealth.

2. Reaction of the Pharisees

The Pharisees, described as lovers of money, ridicule Jesus.
Jesus states that God knows people’s hearts and that what is highly valued by humans may be detestable to God.

He adds that:

  • The Law and the Prophets were proclaimed until John.
  • Since then, the kingdom of God is being announced.
  • It is easier for heaven and earth to disappear than for one stroke of the Law to fail.
  • A brief statement is given regarding divorce and remarriage.

3. Parable of the Rich Man and Lazarus

Jesus tells another parable:

Earthly Conditions

  • A rich man lives in luxury.
  • A poor man named Lazarus lies at his gate, covered in sores, longing for discarded food.
  • Dogs lick Lazarus’s sores.

After Death

  • Lazarus dies and is carried to a place of comfort with Abraham.
  • The rich man dies and is buried; he finds himself in torment.

Conversation Across the Divide

  • The rich man asks Abraham to send Lazarus to cool his tongue with water.
  • Abraham states that the rich man received good things in life while Lazarus received hardship, and now their situations are reversed.
  • A fixed barrier prevents crossing between the two realms.

Request for Warning

  • The rich man asks Abraham to send Lazarus to warn his five brothers.
  • Abraham states that they have Moses and the prophets.
  • The rich man argues that someone returning from the dead would convince them.
  • Abraham responds that if they do not listen to Moses and the prophets, they will not be persuaded even if someone rises from the dead.

 

 

Luke 17 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Warnings About Causing Others to Stumble

Jesus tells his disciples that temptations to sin will occur, but those who cause others to stumble face serious consequences.
He states that it would be preferable to have a heavy stone tied around one’s neck and be thrown into the sea than to cause a vulnerable person to fall into sin.

He instructs them to rebuke a brother who sins and forgive him if he repents—even repeatedly in the same day.

2. Faith and Duty

The apostles ask Jesus to increase their faith.
Jesus replies that even faith the size of a mustard seed can accomplish significant results.

He gives an illustration about servants:

  • A servant who completes fieldwork is not immediately invited to sit and eat.
  • Instead, he prepares the master’s meal first.
  • The servant is not praised for doing what is expected.

Jesus concludes that when his followers obey, they should view themselves as simply fulfilling their duty.

3. Healing of Ten Lepers

Location: Border region between Samaria and Galilee
As Jesus enters a village, ten men with leprosy call out from a distance, asking for mercy.

Jesus instructs them to show themselves to the priests.
As they go, they are healed.

One man—a Samaritan—returns to thank Jesus loudly and falls at his feet.
Jesus notes that only this foreigner returned to give thanks.
He tells the man that his faith has made him well.

4. Questions About the Kingdom of God

Pharisees ask when God’s kingdom will come.
Jesus states that:

  • The kingdom does not arrive with visible signs.
  • The kingdom is already present among them.

He tells his disciples that:

  • A time is coming when they will long to see one of the days of the Son of Man but will not.
  • They should not follow claims that he is in a particular location.
  • His return will be sudden and visible, like lightning across the sky.

He adds that he must first suffer and be rejected.

5. Conditions Before the Son of Man’s Return

Jesus compares future conditions to:

  • The days of Noah, when people continued normal activities until the flood.
  • The days of Lot, when people continued normal activities until Sodom was destroyed.

He states that on the day of the Son of Man’s revealing:

  • People should not return to retrieve belongings.
  • Those who try to preserve their life will lose it; those who lose their life will keep it.

He describes scenarios where two people are together but only one is taken.

6. Question About the Location

The disciples ask where these events will occur.
Jesus responds that where the body is, the vultures will gather—indicating that the events will be obvious when they happen.

 

 

Luke 18 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Parable of the Persistent Widow

Jesus tells a parable to illustrate the need for continual prayer:

  • A widow repeatedly asks a local judge for justice.
  • The judge does not fear God or care about public opinion.
  • He eventually grants her request to avoid further inconvenience.

Jesus states that if an unjust judge responds to persistence, God will certainly respond to his people who call to him.
He adds that when the Son of Man comes, he will look for genuine faith.

2. Parable of the Pharisee and the Tax Collector

Jesus tells another parable directed at those confident in their own righteousness:

  • A Pharisee prays, listing his religious practices and comparing himself favorably to others.
  • A tax collector stands at a distance, asks for mercy, and acknowledges his sin.

Jesus states that the tax collector, not the Pharisee, leaves justified.
He concludes that those who exalt themselves will be humbled, and those who humble themselves will be exalted.

3. Jesus and the Little Children

People bring infants to Jesus for blessing.
The disciples attempt to stop them.
Jesus instructs them to allow the children to come, stating that God’s kingdom belongs to those who receive it with childlike trust.

4. The Rich Ruler

A ruler asks Jesus what must be done to inherit eternal life.
Jesus lists several commandments.
The ruler states he has kept them since youth.

Jesus tells him to sell his possessions, give to the poor, and follow him.
The man becomes sad because he is wealthy.

Jesus states that it is difficult for the rich to enter God’s kingdom, using the comparison of a camel and a needle’s eye.
When asked who can be saved, Jesus replies that what is impossible for humans is possible with God.

Peter notes that the disciples have left everything.
Jesus states that those who leave family or possessions for God’s kingdom will receive greater reward now and in the age to come.

5. Third Prediction of Jesus’ Death

Jesus takes the twelve aside and states:

  • He will go to Jerusalem.
  • Everything written about the Son of Man will be fulfilled.
  • He will be handed over, mocked, insulted, beaten, and killed.
  • He will rise on the third day.

The disciples do not understand the meaning.

6. Healing a Blind Beggar Near Jericho

As Jesus approaches Jericho, a blind man sits by the roadside begging.
He hears a crowd passing and asks what is happening.
He is told that Jesus of Nazareth is passing by.

The man calls out for mercy.
People tell him to be quiet, but he continues calling.

Jesus stops and asks for the man to be brought to him.
Jesus asks what he wants.
The man requests sight.
Jesus states that his faith has healed him.
The man immediately receives sight and follows Jesus, praising God.
The crowd also praises God.

 

 

Luke 19 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Zacchaeus the Tax Collector

Location: Jericho
A chief tax collector named Zacchaeus wants to see Jesus but cannot because of the crowd and his short stature.
He climbs a sycamore tree to get a better view.

Jesus stops, calls him by name, and tells him he will stay at his house.
Zacchaeus welcomes him gladly.
Some observers complain that Jesus is associating with a sinner.

Zacchaeus states that he will give half his possessions to the poor and repay fourfold anyone he has cheated.
Jesus declares that salvation has come to the house and states that the Son of Man came to seek and save the lost.

2. Parable of the Ten Minas

Jesus tells a parable because people expect God’s kingdom to appear immediately:

  • A nobleman travels to a distant country to receive royal authority.
  • Before leaving, he gives ten servants one mina each and instructs them to conduct business.
  • Some citizens oppose his rule.

When he returns with royal authority:

  • One servant reports earning ten minas; he is given authority over ten cities.
  • Another earns five minas; he is given authority over five cities.
  • A third hides his mina out of fear; the nobleman rebukes him and gives his mina to the servant with ten.

Jesus concludes that those who use what they have will receive more, and those who do not will lose even what they have.

3. Triumphal Entry Into Jerusalem

Jesus approaches Jerusalem.
He sends two disciples to retrieve a colt that has never been ridden.
They find it as described and bring it to him.

Jesus rides the colt toward the city.
Crowds spread cloaks on the road and praise God for the miracles they have seen, calling him the king who comes in God’s name.

Some Pharisees tell Jesus to rebuke the crowd.
Jesus replies that if the people were silent, the stones would cry out.

4. Jesus Weeps Over Jerusalem

As Jesus approaches the city, he weeps and states that Jerusalem does not recognize what would bring peace.
He predicts that enemies will surround the city, destroy it, and not leave one stone on another because it did not recognize the time of God’s visitation.

5. Cleansing of the Temple

Jesus enters the temple courts and drives out those selling goods.
He states that the temple is meant to be a house of prayer but has been turned into a den of robbers.

6. Growing Tension With Religious Leaders

Jesus teaches daily in the temple.
The chief priests, scribes, and leaders seek a way to kill him but cannot find an opportunity because the people are attentive to his teaching.

 

 

Luke 20 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Question About Jesus’ Authority

While Jesus is teaching in the temple courts, chief priests, scribes, and elders approach him.
They ask by what authority he acts.

Jesus responds with a counter‑question about whether John’s baptism was from heaven or from humans.
They refuse to answer because either response would put them at risk with the crowd or undermine their challenge.
Jesus then declines to answer their original question.

2. Parable of the Wicked Tenants

Jesus tells a parable:

  • A man plants a vineyard and leases it to tenants before traveling.
  • At harvest time, he sends servants to collect fruit.
  • The tenants beat the first servant, mistreat the second, and injure the third.
  • The owner sends his son, assuming they will respect him.
  • The tenants kill the son, hoping to seize the inheritance.

Jesus asks what the owner will do.
He states that the owner will remove the tenants and give the vineyard to others.
Listeners react strongly.

Jesus quotes a statement about a rejected stone becoming the cornerstone.
He adds that those who oppose it will be broken or crushed.

The religious leaders understand the parable is directed at them and look for a way to arrest him but fear the crowd.

3. Question About Paying Taxes

Spies are sent to trap Jesus with a political question:
“Is it lawful to pay taxes to Caesar?”

Jesus asks for a coin and points out Caesar’s image on it.
He states: “Give to Caesar what belongs to Caesar, and to God what belongs to God.”
The questioners fail to trap him and fall silent.

4. Question About the Resurrection

Sadducees, who deny resurrection, present a scenario involving seven brothers who each marry the same woman in succession after each brother dies.
They ask whose wife she will be in the resurrection.

Jesus replies that marriage does not apply in the resurrection.
He states that those considered worthy of resurrection cannot die anymore and are like angels.
He cites Moses’ reference to God as the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, indicating that God is God of the living.

Some scribes respond positively to his answer.

5. Question About the Messiah

Jesus asks how the Messiah can be David’s son if David calls him “Lord.”
He quotes David’s statement about the Lord inviting his Lord to sit at his right hand.
The implication is that the Messiah is greater than David.

6. Warning About the Scribes

Jesus warns the crowd about the scribes, describing their desire for public honor, best seats, and long prayers used for appearance.
He states that they exploit widows and will receive severe judgment.

 

 

Luke 21 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. The Widow’s Offering

Jesus observes people placing gifts into the temple treasury.
Wealthy individuals contribute large amounts.
A poor widow places in two small coins.

Jesus states that the widow has given more than the others because she contributed out of her poverty, giving all she had to live on.

2. Prediction of the Temple’s Destruction

Some people comment on the beauty of the temple and its decorations.
Jesus states that the time will come when the structure will be destroyed and not one stone will remain on another.

3. Signs and Warnings

People ask when these events will occur and what signs will precede them.

Jesus provides several warnings:

  • Many will come claiming his identity; followers should not be misled.
  • Wars and uprisings will occur, but these are not the immediate end.
  • Nations and kingdoms will rise against each other.
  • There will be earthquakes, famines, and plagues.
  • Fearful events and signs will appear in the sky.

4. Persecution of Followers

Jesus states that before these events:

  • His followers will be arrested, persecuted, and brought before authorities.
  • This will provide opportunities to testify.
  • They should not prepare defensive speeches in advance; he will give them wisdom.
  • They will be betrayed by family members and hated because of their association with him.
  • Not a hair of their head will ultimately be lost; endurance will lead to life.

5. Siege of Jerusalem

Jesus describes a future siege:

  • When Jerusalem is surrounded by armies, its destruction is near.
  • People in Judea should flee to the mountains.
  • Those inside the city should leave; those outside should not enter.
  • This will be a time of judgment.
  • There will be distress, and many will fall by the sword or be taken captive.
  • Jerusalem will be dominated by non‑Jewish nations until their time is complete.

6. Cosmic Signs and the Coming of the Son of Man

Jesus states that:

  • There will be signs in the sun, moon, and stars.
  • Nations will be in confusion over roaring seas and waves.
  • People will faint from fear of what is coming.

He says the Son of Man will appear in a cloud with power and glory.
When these things begin, followers should stand firm because their redemption is near.

7. Parable of the Fig Tree

Jesus uses a fig tree and other trees as an example:

  • When leaves appear, people know summer is near.
  • Likewise, when these signs occur, God’s kingdom is near.

He states that the current generation will not pass away until these things happen.
He adds that heaven and earth may pass away, but his words will not.

8. Call to Watchfulness

Jesus warns:

  • Do not be weighed down by indulgence, drunkenness, or daily anxieties.
  • The coming events will arrive suddenly, like a trap.
  • People should stay alert and pray for strength to endure.

9. Daily Routine in Jerusalem

Jesus teaches in the temple during the day.
At night he stays on the Mount of Olives.
Crowds gather early each morning to hear him.

 

 

Luke 22 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Plot Against Jesus

The Festival of Unleavened Bread, also called Passover, is approaching.
Chief priests and scribes look for a way to arrest and kill Jesus but want to avoid a public disturbance.

Satan influences Judas Iscariot, one of the twelve.
Judas meets with the religious leaders and agrees to betray Jesus in exchange for payment.
They are pleased and look for an opportunity when Jesus is away from the crowds.

2. Preparation for Passover

Jesus sends Peter and John to prepare the Passover meal.
He gives specific instructions: they will meet a man carrying a water jar who will lead them to a furnished upper room.
They find everything as described and prepare the meal.

3. The Last Supper

Jesus reclines at the table with the apostles.
He states that he has eagerly desired to eat this Passover with them before his suffering.

He distributes bread and wine, identifying them as symbols of his body and blood, representing a new covenant.

He announces that one of them will betray him.
The disciples question one another about who it might be.

4. Dispute About Greatness

A dispute arises among the disciples about who is greatest.
Jesus states that leadership among them must be based on service, not status.
He affirms that they have stood by him in his trials and that they will share in his future kingdom.

5. Prediction of Peter’s Denial

Jesus tells Peter that Satan has requested to test the disciples.
Peter insists he is ready to go to prison or death.
Jesus predicts that Peter will deny him three times before the rooster crows.

6. New Instructions

Jesus reminds them of earlier missions when they lacked supplies yet had no needs.
He now instructs them to take money, bags, and even a sword.
They present two swords; Jesus says that is enough.

7. Prayer on the Mount of Olives

Jesus goes to the Mount of Olives as usual, and the disciples follow.
He instructs them to pray to avoid temptation.

He withdraws a short distance, kneels, and prays for the possibility of avoiding the coming suffering, yet submits to God’s will.
An angel strengthens him.
He prays intensely.

He returns to find the disciples asleep from exhaustion and again urges them to pray.

8. Arrest of Jesus

A crowd arrives, led by Judas.
Judas approaches Jesus to kiss him, signaling the arrest.
Jesus questions the gesture.

One disciple strikes the servant of the high priest and cuts off his ear.
Jesus stops the violence and heals the servant.

Jesus tells the arresting group that they are treating him like a criminal even though he taught openly in the temple.
They arrest him and lead him away.

9. Peter’s Denials

Peter follows at a distance.
In the courtyard, three different individuals identify him as a follower of Jesus.
Peter denies it each time.
Immediately after the third denial, a rooster crows.
Jesus turns and looks at Peter.
Peter leaves and weeps bitterly.

10. Mocking and Beating

Jesus is held overnight.
Guards mock him, beat him, blindfold him, and demand that he identify who struck him.
They insult him repeatedly.

11. Before the Jewish Council

At daybreak, Jesus is brought before the council of elders, chief priests, and scribes.

They ask if he is the Messiah.
Jesus states that they will not accept his answer and that the Son of Man will be seated at God’s right hand.

They ask if he is the Son of God.
Jesus replies that they are saying it.
They conclude that no further testimony is needed.

 

 

Luke 23 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. Jesus Before Pilate

The council brings Jesus to Pilate and accuses him of:

  • Misleading the nation
  • Opposing taxes to Caesar
  • Claiming to be a king

Pilate asks Jesus if he is the king of the Jews.
Jesus gives a brief affirmation.
Pilate states he finds no basis for a charge.

The accusers insist Jesus is stirring up unrest from Galilee to Jerusalem.
When Pilate hears “Galilee,” he sends Jesus to Herod, who is in Jerusalem.

2. Jesus Before Herod

Herod is pleased to see Jesus, hoping for a miracle.
He questions Jesus, who does not respond.
Chief priests and scribes accuse him strongly.

Herod and his soldiers mock Jesus, dress him in an elegant robe, and send him back to Pilate.
Herod and Pilate, previously hostile toward each other, become friends that day.

3. Pilate’s Decision

Pilate tells the crowd that neither he nor Herod found Jesus guilty of a capital offense.
He proposes to punish Jesus and release him.

The crowd rejects this and demands the release of Barabbas, a man imprisoned for insurrection and murder.
They shout for Jesus to be crucified.

Pilate attempts to release Jesus again, but the crowd becomes more insistent.
Pilate finally grants their demand:

  • Barabbas is released.
  • Jesus is handed over to be crucified.

4. On the Way to the Crucifixion

As Jesus is led away, soldiers seize Simon of Cyrene and force him to carry the cross behind Jesus.

A large crowd follows, including women who mourn.
Jesus tells them to weep for themselves and their children, predicting future hardship.

5. The Crucifixion

Location: The Skull (Golgotha)
Jesus is crucified along with two criminals, one on each side.

Jesus asks for forgiveness for those carrying out the execution.

Soldiers divide his clothing by casting lots.
Leaders mock him, saying he should save himself if he is the Messiah.
Soldiers offer him sour wine and mockingly call him “King of the Jews.”
A sign above him reads: “This is the King of the Jews.”

One criminal insults Jesus.
The other rebukes the first, acknowledges Jesus’ innocence, and asks to be remembered.
Jesus tells him he will be with him in paradise.

6. Death of Jesus

From noon until mid‑afternoon, darkness covers the land.

The temple curtain tears in two.
Jesus calls out loudly and dies.

A Roman centurion declares that Jesus was righteous.
The crowd, seeing what happened, leaves in distress.
Jesus’ acquaintances and the women who followed him watch from a distance.

7. Burial of Jesus

A council member named Joseph, from Arimathea, who disagreed with the council’s decision, asks Pilate for Jesus’ body.

He takes the body down, wraps it in linen, and places it in a new tomb cut into rock.
It is Preparation Day, just before the Sabbath.

The women who followed Jesus observe the tomb and how the body is laid.
They prepare spices and perfumes but rest on the Sabbath according to the commandment.

 

 

Luke 24 — Matter‑of‑Fact Report

1. The Empty Tomb

Early on the first day of the week, several women go to the tomb with prepared spices.
They find the stone rolled away and the tomb empty.

Two men in radiant clothing appear and tell them that Jesus has risen, reminding them of his earlier predictions.
The women return and report this to the eleven and the others.
Their report is dismissed as unlikely, but Peter runs to the tomb, sees the linen cloths, and leaves wondering what happened.

2. The Road to Emmaus

Two followers travel to a village called Emmaus.
Jesus joins them, but they do not recognize him.
They discuss recent events, including Jesus’ death and the women’s report of the empty tomb.

Jesus explains how the Scriptures point to the Messiah’s suffering and glory.
When they reach the village, they urge him to stay.
At the meal, Jesus breaks bread, and they recognize him.
He disappears immediately.

They return to Jerusalem and report the encounter to the disciples, who state that Jesus has also appeared to Simon.

3. Jesus Appears to the Disciples

While the group is discussing these events, Jesus appears among them and greets them.
They are frightened, thinking they are seeing a spirit.

Jesus shows his hands and feet and invites them to touch him.
He eats a piece of fish to demonstrate he is physically present.

He explains that everything written about him in the Law, Prophets, and Psalms had to be fulfilled.
He states that:

  • The Messiah would suffer and rise on the third day.
  • Repentance and forgiveness of sins would be proclaimed in his name to all nations, beginning in Jerusalem.
  • They are witnesses of these things.

He promises that he will send what the Father has promised and instructs them to stay in the city until they receive power from above.

4. The Ascension

Jesus leads them to the vicinity of Bethany.
He lifts his hands and blesses them.
While blessing them, he is taken up from them.

The disciples return to Jerusalem with joy and continually gather in the temple praising God.
